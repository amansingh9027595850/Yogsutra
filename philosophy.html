Ancient Roots

The Philosophy of Union

Yoga is more than alignment of bone and muscle. It is a comprehensive framework for living consciously and finding union within.

What is Yoga?

Derived from the Sanskrit word "Yuj," meaning to yoke, unite, or bind.

Historically, the practice of yoga was formulated to quiet the analytical chatter of the conscious mind, returning the individual to their essential, calm state. In the classic yoga treatise, the Yoga Sutras of Patanjali, yoga is defined as: "Yogash chitta vritti nirodhah" — the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind.

To achieve this quietness, Patanjali organized a comprehensive eight-fold path. This guide consists of eight progressive steps, starting with ethical social behaviors and moving inward to deep meditation.

The Yamas: Social Ethics

The Yamas deal with one's ethical standards and sense of integrity, focusing on how we behave toward others. There are five Yamas:

  • Ahimsa: Non-violence or non-harming (practicing kindness to all beings, including yourself).
  • Satya: Truthfulness (speaking and living truth with compassion).
  • Asteya: Non-stealing (not taking what is not freely offered).
  • Brahmacharya: Right use of energy (moderation and conserving energy for spiritual growth).
  • Aparigraha: Non-greed or non-attachment (living simply and letting go of hoarding habits).

The Niyamas: Personal Observances

The Niyamas have to do with self-discipline and inner observances, providing a personal code of conduct. The five Niyamas are:

  • Saucha: Purity (keeping the body, space, and mind clean).
  • Santosha: Contentment (accepting and finding gratitude in the present moment).
  • Tapas: Discipline or heat (burning away blockages through regular practice).
  • Svadhyaya: Self-study (studying sacred texts and observing your own behaviors).
  • Ishvara Pranidhana: Devotion (surrendering to a higher power or the flow of life).

Asana: The Physical Temple

Asanas are the physical postures practiced in modern yoga classes. Traditionally, Patanjali designed physical postures to build energy, restore cellular health, and prepare the physical body to sit in silent meditation for hours without distraction.

Pranayama: Regulating the Life Force

Pranayama consists of breathing techniques designed to gain mastery over the respiratory process while recognizing the connection between the breath, the mind, and the nervous system. Regulating your breath lets you directly influence your stress levels.

Pratyahara: Turning Inward

Pratyahara is the practice of drawing our attention away from external distractions, sounds, and physical sensations. By turning the sensory receptors inward, we gain distance from cravings and emotional reactions.

The Inner Limbs of Meditation

The final three limbs represent the inward journey toward self-realization:

  • Dharana (Concentration): Fixing the mind on a single focus point (like the breath, a mantra, or a candle flame).
  • Dhyana (Meditation): The uninterrupted flow of concentration where the mind merges into the object of focus.
  • Samadhi (Liberation): A state of pure ecstatic union, transcending the ego, and merging with the universal consciousness.
